\section{Problem focus}

This note records the current architecture study for the $L=4$, $p=4$ lost-sales family. The
central question is not whether one policy class is universally optimal, but which policy
parameterizations expose a replenishment rule that CMA-ES can search reliably.

We study two closely related problems:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{vanilla lost sales}: no fixed setup cost,
    \item \textbf{fixed-cost lost sales}: a positive fixed ordering cost $K$ is charged whenever
    $q_t > 0$.
\end{itemize}

In both cases the controller observes the current inventory state and chooses an integer order
quantity $q_t \in \{0,1,\ldots,\bar q\}$. Demand is stochastic and unmet demand is lost. The
fixed-cost version adds the term $K\mathbf{1}\{q_t>0\}$ to the one-period cost, which makes the
order/no-order boundary much more important.

\section{Current benchmark instances and protocol}

The current single-instance study uses:
\begin{itemize}
    \item vanilla instance \texttt{vanilla\_l4\_p4\_poisson5},
    \item fixed-cost instance \texttt{lit\_pois\_mu5\_l4\_p4\_k5}.
\end{itemize}

Their shared core parameters are:
\[
L = 4, \qquad p = 4, \qquad h = 1, \qquad \mu = 5.
\]
The fixed-cost instance additionally uses $K=5$.

All current runs in this note use the same training budget:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $2000$ CMA-ES iterations,
    \item population size $64$,
    \item training horizon $2000$,
    \item evaluation horizon $10^6$,
    \item $10$ evaluation seeds,
    \item seed $42$.
\end{itemize}

For the demand-robustness sweep we use four mean-preserving demand families:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Poisson demand with mean $5$,
    \item Geometric demand with mean $5$,
    \item positively correlated two-state Markov-modulated Poisson demand with
    $(\lambda_{\mathrm{low}}, \lambda_{\mathrm{high}})=(3,7)$ and $p_{00}=p_{11}=0.9$,
    \item negatively correlated two-state Markov-modulated Poisson demand with
    $(\lambda_{\mathrm{low}}, \lambda_{\mathrm{high}})=(3,7)$ and $p_{00}=p_{11}=0.1$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Policy families and action equations}

The current study is intentionally narrow: it focuses on linear policies and soft trees. Let
$z = w^\top x + b$ denote the scalar latent score of a linear head, and let $Q_{\mathrm{env}}$ be
the environment action cap.

We study the following action parameterizations:

\paragraph{Categorical quantity.}
The policy emits $Q_{\mathrm{env}}+1$ logits and chooses
\[
a_t = \arg\max_{a \in \{0,1,\ldots,Q_{\mathrm{env}}\}} z_a(x_t).
\]

\paragraph{Sigmoid direct quantity.}
The policy emits one scalar and maps it as
\[
a_t = \mathrm{round}\!\left(Q_{\mathrm{env}} \, \sigma(z)\right).
\]
This is a one-head bounded S-curve map.

\paragraph{Direct quantity.}
The policy emits one scalar and maps it as
\[
\tilde a_t = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\mathrm{softplus}(z)\right),
\qquad
a_t = \mathrm{clip}(\tilde a_t, 0, Q_{\mathrm{env}}).
\]
This is a one-head positive-part map with no explicit $Q_{\mathrm{env}}$ inside the head.

\paragraph{Soft-gated sigmoid direct quantity.}
The policy emits a gate score $g(x_t)$ and a quantity score $q(x_t)$, then sets
\[
a_t = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\sigma(g(x_t)) \, \sigma(q(x_t)) \, Q_{\mathrm{env}}\right).
\]

\paragraph{Soft-gated direct quantity.}
The policy emits $g(x_t)$ and $q(x_t)$, then sets
\[
\tilde a_t = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\sigma(g(x_t)) \, \mathrm{softplus}(q(x_t))\right),
\qquad
a_t = \mathrm{clip}(\tilde a_t, 0, Q_{\mathrm{env}}).
\]

\paragraph{Hard-gated direct quantity.}
The policy emits $g(x_t)$ and $q(x_t)$, and uses
\[
a_t =
\begin{cases}
0, & \sigma(g(x_t)) < 0.5, \\
\mathrm{clip}(\mathrm{round}(\mathrm{softplus}(q(x_t))), 1, Q_{\mathrm{env}}),
& \sigma(g(x_t)) \ge 0.5.
\end{cases}
\]

\paragraph{Soft-gated ordinal quantity.}
The policy emits one gate score $g(x_t)$ and ordinal scores
$o_1(x_t),\ldots,o_{Q_{\mathrm{env}}}(x_t)$, and sets
\[
\hat m(x_t) = \sum_{k=1}^{Q_{\mathrm{env}}} \sigma(o_k(x_t)),
\qquad
a_t = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\sigma(g(x_t)) \, \hat m(x_t)\right).
\]

\paragraph{Soft trees with linear leaves.}
A soft tree computes path probabilities $\pi_\ell(x_t)$ over leaves, and each leaf emits a local
linear quantity model
\[
q_\ell(x_t) = \mathrm{softplus}(\alpha_\ell^\top x_t + \beta_\ell).
\]
The final action is the rounded mixture
\[
\tilde a_t = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\sum_\ell \pi_\ell(x_t) q_\ell(x_t)\right),
\qquad
a_t = \mathrm{clip}(\tilde a_t, 0, Q_{\mathrm{env}}).
\]
We use depth-$1$ and depth-$2$ oblique soft trees.

The policy family that is \emph{not} part of the active benchmark surface is the raw unbounded
direct head
\[
a_t = \mathrm{clip}(\mathrm{round}(z), 0, Q_{\mathrm{env}}),
\]
which repeatedly converged to weak basins in earlier runs and is now treated as a negative ablation
rather than a serious candidate.

\begin{itemize}

    \item the $Q$-free soft-gated direct policy is good,
    \item the trees are good.
\end{itemize}
So the real issue is search geometry, not a single symbolic design choice.

This is the clearest current architecture result in the fixed-cost setting:

\begin{itemize}
    \item there is no single winner across all fixed-cost demand families,
    \item the best head depends on the demand geometry,
    \item structured direct heads and trees consistently beat the best heuristic,
    \item flat categorical quantity remains weak.
\end{itemize}

The pattern is also suggestive:
\begin{itemize}
    \item positively correlated and overdispersed fixed-cost demand favor harder thresholded direct
    heads,
    \item negatively correlated fixed-cost demand favors the soft-gated direct head,
    \item Poisson fixed-cost demand still slightly favors the trees.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Vanilla sweep}

The vanilla demand sweep is still in progress. At the time of writing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Poisson is complete,
    \item Geometric is complete,
    \item the positively correlated MMPP2 case has started,
    \item the negatively correlated MMPP2 case has not yet been summarized into the current
    manuscript snapshot.
\end{itemize}

For the completed vanilla demand cases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Poisson best learned policy: linear gated sigmoid direct, cost $4.7451$ versus heuristic
    $4.8168$,
    \item Geometric best learned policy so far: depth-$1$ soft tree, cost $10.6391$ versus
    heuristic $10.8025$.
\end{itemize}

The vanilla side is therefore currently much less architecture-sensitive than the fixed-cost side.
Once we move away from the flat categorical head, almost all of the direct/tree families appear to
reach essentially the same good basin.

\section{Interpretation}

The original local hypothesis was that \emph{factorization} or \emph{gating} might be the main
winning ingredient. The current evidence is weaker and more precise than that.

What we can say now is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The raw unbounded direct head is a bad search parameterization and should remain excluded
    from the active benchmark surface.
    \item The one-head sigmoid direct map is not fundamentally broken; it is search-sensitive. It
    can be bad at smaller populations and good at larger ones.
    \item The fixed-cost problem remains much more architecture-sensitive than the vanilla problem.
    \item The most stable current interpretation is that these policy families differ mainly in the
    replenishment geometry they expose to CMA-ES, not only in raw expressivity.
\end{enumerate}

So the best current reading is:
\begin{quote}
Good policies are the ones whose parameterization makes the relevant replenishment map easy enough
for CMA-ES to find under the available budget.
\end{quote}

That map need not always be represented in the same way. In vanilla lost sales it can be realized
by many non-categorical direct and tree heads. In fixed-cost lost sales it is still much more
dependent on the action-head geometry and the demand family.

\section{Published verification anchor}

Before relying on the fixed-cost simulator and heuristic stack for learned-policy comparisons, we
validate the implementation against the published example used in
\citet{Bijvank2015ParametricPolicies}. Their Table~1 reports a small Poisson-demand instance with:
\[
L=2,\qquad p=14,\qquad K=5,\qquad h=1,\qquad \mu=5.
\]

In the repo, this validation anchor is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{bijvank2015\_table1\_l2\_p14\_k5}
\end{itemize}
and is encoded in
\texttt{invman/problems/lost\_sales\_fixed\_order\_cost/reference\_instances.py}.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Published fixed-cost lost-sales validation example from
\citet{Bijvank2015ParametricPolicies} and our reproduced long-run costs for the reported heuristic
parameters.}

\toprule

The reproduced costs match the published values to within simulation precision. This is the
literature-backed correctness anchor for the fixed-cost environment and heuristic implementation.

It is important to distinguish this validation role from the architecture study above:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the validation instance is used to verify that the simulator and heuristic policies match
    the published benchmark family,
    \item the canonical $L=4$, $p=4$ studies are where we compare modern learned-policy
    parameterizations.
\end{itemize}
