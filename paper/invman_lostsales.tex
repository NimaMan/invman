\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates the use of Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategies (CMA-ES) for solving the lost sales inventory problem, a critical challenge in inventory management. Traditional approaches, such as heuristics and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), often struggle with large-scale problems or require extensive hyperparameter tuning. We propose CMA-ES as an alternative to conventional DRL methods for optimizing neural network-based inventory policies. Unlike typical DRL approaches, our method emphasizes optimizing overall policy performance rather than focusing on individual action-value estimation, simplifying the optimization process and effectively managing the exploration-exploitation trade-off.

Our results show that CMA-ES can efficiently discover high-performing policies with significantly fewer parameters, reducing the need for complex tuning and enhancing interpretability. We find that CMA-ES not only competes with traditional heuristics and leading DRL algorithms but also performs particularly well in settings characterized by lower shortage costs and longer lead times. Additionally, we show that CMA-ES can train competitive policies using simple linear models, further highlighting its efficiency and practicality. This research offers a new, effective, and interpretable approach to tackling complex inventory management problems, with potential applications across a wide range of decision-making challenges in operations management.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Inventory management is a fundamental challenge in the field of operations management. Inventory problems involve sequential decision making on when and how much inventory to order to meet customer demand, while minimizing the costs associated with inventory underage and overage. Many inventory problems can be formulated as Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), and while exact algorithms solve small problem instances efficiently, they fail in solving larger instances in a reasonable amount of time due to the curse of dimensionality. Hence, researchers have designed heuristics to solve larger-scale problems, typically making use of the structural properties of the problem. Examples include base-stock policies, $(s,Q)$ policies, and $(s,S)$ policies. Designing heuristics requires significant specialized knowledge about the problem. In addition, researchers usually make simplifying assumptions about the problem to come up with these heuristics, which limits their applicability in practice. In this paper, we assess inventory policies obtained using machine learning approaches that are readily applicable to a wide range of inventory problems, also for problems for which no heuristics are available. In order to gain reliable insights into this approach, the focal problem in this study is a quintessential, difficult inventory problem for which very effective heuristics are available: the lost sales problem \citep{morton1971near,Zipkin2008OldSystems,Bijvank2011Lost-salesReview}.

Recently, Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has achieved remarkable success in solving complex problems, particularly in the domain of games, as demonstrated by breakthroughs such as AlphaGo \citep{Silver2016MasteringSearch, Silver2017MasteringKnowledge}. These successes have spurred interest in applying DRL to other fields, including inventory management. For instance, \cite{Gijsbrechts2022CanProblems} show that it is possible to train a DRL agent to effectively address inventory control problems like the lost sales problem. In the DRL framework, ordering policies are represented as neural networks with tunable parameters, and the primary objective is to optimize these parameters to develop a high-performing policy \citep[see][]{boute2022deep}. Despite its potential, the application of DRL in inventory management faces significant challenges. Training DRL models often requires considerable computational resources and extensive hyperparameter tuning—fine-tuning the settings of the learning algorithm itself—to achieve good performance \citep[see][]{Gijsbrechts2022CanProblems}. Moreover, the neural networks employed typically contain thousands of parameters, making the resulting policies difficult to interpret and apply in practice.

In this paper, we propose to adopt an alternative optimization method for learning the parameters of neural network inventory policies, namely Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategies (CMA-ES). CMA-ES is an Evolution Strategy that performs a structured exploration of the parameter space of the policy function using its covariance matrix \citep{Salimans2017EvolutionLearning}. With CMA-ES, we take a very different approach to policy optimization compared to typical DRL algorithms such as A3C and PPO \citep{mnih2016asynchronous,schulman2017proximal}: Instead of explicitly recording individual actions taken in simulation trajectories, CMA-ES only concerns itself with overall performance over the entire trajectory. CMA-ES inherently incorporates an exploration mechanism. This is in contrast to DRL, where devising an effective exploration strategy often requires careful design and adjustment. This automatic exploration significantly simplifies the optimization process, as it inherently navigates through the parameter space, efficiently balancing the exploration-exploitation trade-off without the need for explicit strategy formulation.

As a key contribution of this paper, we demonstrate how CMA-ES can be used to train well-performing neural network policies for the lost sales inventory problem. We find that CMA-ES can relatively quickly identify well-performing policies without the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning. These policies quite consistently outperform base-stock policies. Moreover, CMA-ES can achieve $3-4\%$ lower average costs compared to the best performing traditional heuristics such as the Myopic-2 and Capped Base-Stock policies for problems with a low penalty cost and long lead times. CMA-ES has similar performance for problems with a low penalty cost and short lead time. %, but it is outperformed in cases with a high penalty cost.
Finally, without any hyperparameter optimization, the CMA-ES method tends to deliver better performing policies than those obtained with well-known state-of-the-art model-free DRL algorithms such as those reported in literature for A3C \citep{Gijsbrechts2022CanProblems}. Linear and neural network policy function approximators trained using CMA-ES perform better than the policies trained in \citep{Gijsbrechts2022CanProblems}, and are orders of magnitude smaller. Since our proposed methodology is quite generic, it can be applied to other inventory management problems with similar decision-making structures.

Perhaps equally importantly, we contribute by finding that CMA-ES can identify competitive neural network policies even while the neural network has very few parameters/weights. In particular, whereas the policy function approximator in typical DRL applications in inventory control involves several hidden layers and tens of thousands of parameters/weights \citep{Gijsbrechts2022CanProblems,kaynov2024deep,wang2024deep, dehaybe2024deep}, we find that CMA-ES can train competitive policies based on a much simpler design without hidden layers, and with less than one hundred parameters/weights: A \emph{linear policy function}. To understand how such a policy selects an action, suppose that a vector $\mathbf{s}$ represents the dynamic state of the lost sales inventory system: $\mathbf{s}$ contains the information on the on-hand inventory and the inventory pipeline. Then, the \emph{score} for a specific order quantity $q$ is computed as $\mathbf{w}_q \cdot \mathbf{s} + b_q$, where $\textbf{w}_q$ and $b_q$ for $q\in \{0,1,\ldots,\bar{q}\}$ are parameters that will be learned with CMA-ES, and $\bar{q}$ represents a provable upper bound on the optimal order quantity \citep[see e.g.][]{Zipkin2008OldSystems}. When placing an order, we compute the score for each possible order quantity, and select the quantity with the highest score. While being more involved than heuristics such as base-stock policies, the resulting linear order policy is arguably more interpretable than a neural network with one or several hidden layers. Apart from interpretability, we believe it is theoretically interesting that such a small network can represent policies as good as a neural network.

Furthermore, we propose a visualization technique that provides insights into the decision-making process of the linear policy function approximator. By examining the relationship between the current inventory level and the sum of previously ordered products, as well as the individual dimensions of the state space, we uncover clear patterns in the policy's behavior. The visualizations reveal how the policy adapts its order quantities based on the current state of the system and the expected future supply, effectively balancing the need for replenishment with the risk of overstocking. This interpretability aspect of the linear policy function approximator is crucial for operational settings where understanding the rationale behind decisions is as important as the decisions themselves.

\section{Literature}
\label{lit}

In this paper, we learn linear and neural network ordering policies for inventory problems using a form of machine learning: Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES). We first discuss the relatively recent adoption of neural network inventory policies, and DRL algorithms for training those policies. Then we discuss other machine learning methods for training neural network policies, including CMA-ES.

Many classical inventory problems can be trivially modelled as a MDP, but those MDPs are often computationally intractable because of the curse of dimensionality. There are several approaches for arriving at satisfactory solutions for such intractable MDPs, such as problem-specific analytical \emph{heuristics}, e.g. $(S,Q)$ policies. To overcome limitations of such heuristics, recent research has proposed to adopt DRL for inventory control. A key idea in DRL is the use of neural networks to represent policies and/or value functions \citep{boute2022deep}. Because neural networks can represent a very wide range of functions, their adoption potentially eliminates the need for defining appropriate problem specific strategies: To represent an inventory policy as a neural network, states of the MDP are interpreted as vectors that will be used as input to the neural network, while the output will be interpreted as a distribution over the possible actions, e.g. order sizes, see \cite{boute2022deep} for a detailed discussion.

DRL is regarded as a promising research direction for inventory applications, and a range of Reinforcement Learning algorithms has been successfully applied to a variety of problems. Deep Q Networks (DQN) is amongst the earliest DRL algorithms, and is successfully applied to the beer game \citep{oroojlooyjadid2022deep} and to perishable inventory control \citep{demoor2022reward}. The flexibility of DRL algorithms to adapt to different problems was convincingly demonstrated with the application of A3C to dual sourcing, lost sales, and one-warehouse multi-retailer problems \citep{Gijsbrechts2022CanProblems}. Recent work is increasingly adopting proximal policy optimization (PPO), for example for the joint ordering problem \citep{vanvuchelen2020use}, the asymmetric one-warehouse multi-retailer problem \citep{kaynov2024deep}, stochastic capacitated lot sizing \citep{vanhezewijk2023using}, and inventory control for non-stationary stochastic demand \citep{dehaybe2024deep}. \cite{temizoz2025deep} propose a different algorithm that is specifically designed for highly stochastic models, viz. Deep Controlled Learning (DCL), and demonstrate superior results for several classical inventory models \citep[see also][]{van2025scalable}. DQN, A3C, PPO, and DCL, although differing in their specific mechanisms, all utilize state-action-reward sequences as the foundational structure for learning and optimizing policies in Reinforcement Learning environments. That is, these algorithms involve the attribution of rewards obtained over some simulation trajectory to individual actions taken in that trajectory, and subsequently the tuning of neural network parameters to promote the use of actions that are deemed to output favorable actions.

CMA-ES is a global optimization method that has been successfully used for a wide range of contexts \citep{Hansen1996AdaptingAdaptation,Hansen2001CompletelyStrategies.,Hansen2006TheReview}. CMA-ES was not originally proposed as a method for learning neural network policies, but recent work demonstrated its applicability for this purpose \citep{Salimans2017EvolutionLearning}. CMA-ES has fewer hyperparameters and its design inherently encourages exploration, making it potentially easier to tune and more robust. Unlike DRL algorithms, which rely on gradient information and backpropagation, CMA-ES belongs to the broader class of evolutionary algorithms that are population-based and gradient-free, mimicking natural selection through iterative sampling and adaptation. Its ability to adapt the full covariance matrix distinguishes it from simpler Evolution Strategies, enabling more efficient search in complex, high-dimensional spaces, such as the one considered in this paper.

Within the inventory context, CMA-ES and other evolutionary algorithms have been adopted to optimize the parameters of problem-specific heuristics, e.g. $(S,Q)$ policies \citep{Bosman2007InventoryOptimization}. However, we are not aware of applications of evolutionary algorithms to learn generic ordering policies. In fact, the vast majority of machine learning algorithms applied to optimize generic ordering policies have adopted classical DRL algorithms focusing on state-action-reward signals, such as PPO and A3C. We next discuss some salient exceptions.

To optimize inventory control for a large market-place, \cite{madeka2022deep} propose to apply a form of supervised learning. In particular, they directly apply back propagation to a simulation model of an inventory system, based on real-world estimated demand data. \cite{vanderhaar2023supervised} similarly model an inventory problem as a supervised learning problem, but rather than attempting to model the entire problem as a differential simulator, they propose a method to assign costs to each individual decision, extending the applicability of data-driven methods \citep{ban2019big} to cases where it is difficult to find a simple closed-form policy. Finally, \cite{amiri2023adaptive} adopt multi-armed bandit methods and change-point detection for data-driven policy optimization.

Our paper similarly diverges from the standard methods for training generic (neural network) policies, and is, to our knowledge, the first to adopt evolutionary algorithms for the purpose. The learning of generic ordering policies for inventory systems is a nascent field, and as such it benefits from experimentation with a range of methods, to gain a good overview of strengths and limitations of various method, both in terms of performance and in terms of interpretability.


\section{Solving lost sales inventory management problem using policy optimization}
\label{lost_sales}

\subsection{Lost sales inventory management problem}

The lost sales problem is one of the fundamental problems in the inventory management literature \citep{Zipkin2008OldSystems, Bijvank2011Lost-salesReview}. We consider the single-item, periodic review lost sales inventory management problem. The demand and order quantities are assumed to be integer. The objective of the problem is to minimize the long-run average cost of the system, which consists of the lost-sales and inventory holding costs. At the beginning of each period $ t \in \{1, 2, \dots, T \}$, the inventory manager has to decide on the number of products to be ordered $q_t$. (We are mainly interested in the limit $T \rightarrow \infty$, to be discussed in detail below.) The ordered products arrive after $L \geq 1$ periods, i.e., at the start of period $t+L$.

Right before making the ordering decision, $q_{t-L}$ products ordered in period $t-L$ arrive; we let $I_t$ denote the inventory on hand in period $t$ after adding the order placed in period $t-L$, but before the arrival of demand. The realized demand in period $t$, denoted by $d_t$, follows the distribution $D_t$. We assume that the demand distribution is the same across all periods, i.e., $D_t \sim D$ for all $t$. In other words, the demand process is stationary.
The inventory on hand is utilized to meet the arriving demand $d_t$. If not all demand can be fulfilled immediately by inventory on hand, we have lost sales. Consequently, $I_t$ can be expressed as follows:
\begin{equation*}
    I_{t}=\left(I_{t-1}-d_{t-1}\right)^+ + q_{t-L},
\end{equation*}
where $x^+ = \max(0, x)$.

The holding cost for keeping one unit of inventory on hand is denoted by $h$, while the penalty cost for each unit of lost sales is denoted by $p$. We assume that there are no fixed costs associated with placing orders. The total cost incurred in period $t$, denoted by $c_t$, for a given realized demand $d_t$ are thus:
\begin{equation}
    \label{lost_sales_cost}
    c_t = h\left(I_{t}  - d_t\right)^+ + p\left(d_t - I_{t}\right)^+,
    %C_t = h\left(I_{t-1} + q_{t-L} - d_t\right)^+ + p\left(d_t - I_{t-1} - q_{t-L}\right)^+.
\end{equation}
while the expected total cost for period $t$ are:
\begin{equation}
    \label{lost_sales_average_cost}
    \mathbb{E}[c_t] = h \cdot \mathbb{E}[\left(I_{t}  - D_t\right)^+] + p \cdot \mathbb{E}[\left(D_t - I_{t}\right)^+],
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{E}[\cdot]$ denotes the expectation over the demand $D_t$. Denote the average expected costs over a horizon of $T$ periods by $\bar{C}_T(\btheta) = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{E}[c_t]$, where the expectation is taken under policy $\pi_{\btheta}$. The objective of the inventory management problem is to minimize the long-run average expected cost, which is formulated as the limit of $\bar{C}_T(\btheta)$ as the time horizon $T$ approaches infinity:
\begin{equation*}
    \bar{C}(\btheta) = \lim_{T \to \infty} \bar{C}_T(\btheta).
\end{equation*}
%\textcolor{\hl}{It represents the theoretical objective we aim to minimize. We estimate this quantity using finite-horizon simulations, which we later denote as $C(\btheta)$ for a policy parametrized by $\btheta$. }
Note that since demand is stationary, the entire problem is stationary, and hence our objective is to minimize the average expected costs in this inventory system.

We consider periods $t \in \{1, 2, \dots, T\}$ in our formulation, where the infinite horizon average cost criterion that arises as $T \to \infty$ is the focus of this paper. However, note that we propose simulation-based approaches for optimizing linear and neural network policies, and for those purposes, we also evaluate performance over finite horizons. In particular, for training policies, we use smaller horizons for computational efficiency. For evaluating policy performance, we use longer horizons with a warm-up period to approximate infinite-horizon behavior, an approach aligning with the standards in inventory literature.

Our decision variable in this problem is $q_t$, the number of products to be ordered at the beginning of period $t$, for $t \in \{1, 2, \dots, T\}$. In order to find the optimal policy for setting $q_t$, one may formulate the problem as an MDP. Since $q_{t-L}$ products arrive at the beginning of period $t$, one can represent the system state after arrival of this order as:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{S}_t = \left( I_t,q_{t-L+1} \dots, q_{t-1} \right).
    %\left( \left(I_{t-1}-d_{t-1}\right)^+ + q_{t-L}, \dots, q_{t-1} \right),
\end{equation*}
This means we have an $L$-dimensional state-space, while the cost in period $t$ is a function of the first dimension of $\mathbf{S}_t$ only. Notice that while the form of the cost function \eqref{lost_sales_average_cost} appears simple, the starting inventory $I_t$ cannot be controlled directly, but arises as a complex function of the ordering policy, i.e., the method to decide on $q_t$ based on the system state $\mathbf{S}_t$. Moreover, solving the problem as an MDP via policy or value iteration is intractable because the state space grows exponentially in the lead time. Therefore, the lost sales inventory problem is considered to be a difficult optimization problem \citep{Bijvank2011Lost-salesReview}.

The Bellman optimality equation \citep{Bellman1954TheProgramming} of the lost-sales problem can be written as

\subsection{Policy function approximators}
\label{policy_function_app}

There exist several iterative methods for solving an MDP exactly, such as value iteration and policy iteration. However, the exact methods can only be used to solve small-scale problems due to the curse-of-dimensionality. Hence, researchers have relied on approximate methods to solve large-scale MDPs. A popular subclass of approximate approaches is value and policy function approximation using function approximators. Value function approximation methods directly estimate the value of being in a certain state. On the other hand, policy approximation directly parametrizes the behavior of the agent in different situations. It acts as the brain of the agent and describes which action to take in different states of the problem. In this paper, we consider solving the inventory management problem using policy approximation.

Throughout this paper, we use a consistent state-action representation where the state vector $\mathbf{S}_t = (s_{t1}, \dots, s_{tL}) =(s_{1}, \dots, s_{L})$ captures inventory position and pipeline inventory at time $t$, where the dependence on $t$ is sometimes suppressed to avoid  notational overload. Actions correspond directly to order quantities. For each possible state vector $\mathbf{S}_t$, our policy generates \emph{scores} for each possible action; we then select the action which has the highest corresponding score. Note that since our problem is stationary, we are seeking to identify a well-performing stationary policy.

Several policy function approximators exist in the machine learning literature, such as the linear combination of the state features', neural networks, decision trees, and nearest-neighbors. We consider the linear combinations of features and neural network policy approximators. Typical policy function approximators used in Deep Reinforcement Learning \citep{boute2022deep} map the current state of the problem onto the probability of taking the actions in the action-space of the problem; note that we instead use a deterministic policy where each state is mapped to the action with the highest score. Note that we do not need to adopt probabilistic policies, because CMA-ES inherently ensures sufficient exploration.

The action-space of the lost sales problem consists of a set of discrete action in $\{0, 1, \dots, \bar{q}\}$ representing the number of products to be ordered, where $\bar{q}$ is the maximum number of products that can be ordered. The choice of \(\bar{q}\) can be informed by theoretical upper bounds specific to the problem, as suggested by \cite{Zipkin2008OldSystems} for the lost sales problem, or it can be set based on some loose upper bound, such as the 99th percentile of the demand distribution.

A linear policy function approximator determines the so-called \emph{score} (see below) of action $q$ in the action-space by using the dot product of the current state of the system and a set of weights $w_{1, q}, w_{2, q},\dots, w_{L, q}$, plus a bias weight $b_q$ using Equation (\ref{linear_policy_eq}).
\begin{equation}
    \label{linear_policy_eq}
    y_q = s_1 w_{q,1} + s_2w_{q,2} + \ldots + s_Lw_{q,L} + b_q.
\end{equation}
The \emph{score}, \( y_q \), in the context of policy function approximators and particularly for a linear policy function approximator, %refers to a computed value that quantifies the desirability or suitability of a particular action \( q \) given the current state of the system.

current state of the system.

\begin{equation}
\pi_{\btheta}(\mathbf{S}) = \argmax_{q \in \{0, 1, \ldots, \bar{q}\}} y_q, \label{eq:policydef}
\end{equation}
(We break tied scores by selecting the highest action. )

For concreteness in the following example, we set $L=2$ and $\bar{q}=3$, so the action-space is $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ and the state is represented as $\mathbf{S}=(s_1, s_2)$. The scores of each action in the action-space can be calculated as:
\begin{equation}
      \begin{pmatrix}
        y_{0} \\[0.3em]
        y_{1} \\
        y_2 \\
        y_{3}
      \end{pmatrix} =
      \begin{pmatrix}
        w_{0,1} & w_{0,2} \\
        w_{1,1} & w_{1,2} \\
        w_{2,1} & w_{2,2}  \\
        w_{3,1} & w_{3,2}
      \end{pmatrix}
      \begin{pmatrix}
        s_{1} \\[0.3em]
        s_{2}
      \end{pmatrix}
      +
      \begin{pmatrix}
        b_{0} \\[0.3em]
        b_{1} \\
        b_2 \\
        b_{3}
      \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
These scores are then used to select an action by choosing the one with the highest score, according to the argmax rule: $\pi_{\btheta}(\mathbf{S}) = \argmax_{q \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 3\}} y_q$. Concatenating the weights and biases of this function approximator into a one dimensional vector results in a vector of size $4\times2 + 4 = 12$, representing $\btheta$. In general, the parameters of the linear policy function approximator consist of a vector of real numbers of size ${(\bar{q}+1)}\times L + \bar{q}+1 =(\bar{q}+1)\times (L+1) $.

A neural network policy approximator projects the state of the problem into nonlinear hidden layers which then determine the scores of each action. For instance, consider the neural network presented in Figure \ref{nn_policy}. Neural networks use a nonlinear activation function, denoted by $\nu$, which determines the importance of each neuron in determining the score of a certain action. In our numerical experiments, we employ the ``Scaled Exponential Linear Units`` (SELU) activation function, as proposed by \citep{Klambauer2017Self-NormalizingNetworks}. We opted for SELU due to its self-normalizing properties, which help maintain a stable mean and variance of the network's activations. This attribute leads to improved training dynamics and convergence properties compared to traditional activation functions. Preliminary experiments have demonstrated that SELU achieves slightly better results than alternatives such as the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU). It is defined as:
    \begin{equation*}
\operatorname{\nu}(x)=\lambda\left\{\begin{array}{ll}
x & \text { if } x>0, \\
\beta e^{x}-\beta & \text { if } x \leqslant 0,
\end{array}\right.
\end{equation*}
where $\beta \approx 1.6733$ and $\lambda \approx 1.0507$. In a similar fashion to the linear function approximator, the parameters of the neural network are concatenated into a vector to represent $\btheta$. For instance, consider a two-layer neural network with a hidden layer of size $H$ presented in Figure \ref{nn_policy}. $\btheta$ is equivalent to a vector of real numbers of length $L\times H + H + H\times{(\bar{q}+1)} + {\bar{q}+1}$ for this neural network: $L\times H$ weights connecting the input and the hidden layer, $H$ biases for the hidden layer, $H\times (\bar{q}+1)$ weights connecting the hidden and the output layer, and $\bar{q}+1$ biases for the output layer.

\subsection{Solving the lost sales problem using policy function approximators}
Let $\mathbf{S}_1$ demonstrate the initial state of the lost sales problem. We initialize $\mathbf{S}_1$ by ordering a random number of products (less than the maximum order size) for $L$ periods. Then, in period $t$, we use $\pi_{\btheta} (\mathbf{S}_t)$ as defined in \eqref{eq:policydef} to determine the order size $q$, for $t \in \{1, 2, \dots, T\}$. The empirical average cost per period of the problem using policy $\pi_{\btheta}$ over a horizon of length $T$ is then calculated as:
\begin{equation*}
    \hat{C}_T(\btheta) = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} c_t.
\end{equation*}
$\bar{C}_T(\btheta)$ determines the expected performance of parameter vector $\btheta$ in controlling the lost sales problem, and our long-run objective is $\bar{C}(\btheta) = \lim_{T\to\infty} \bar{C}_T(\btheta)$. As it is intractable to evaluate the limit of $T\rightarrow \infty$ for any given $\btheta$, our algorithms estimate $\bar{C}_T(\btheta)$ using simulation samples $\hat{C}_T(\btheta)$, and we effectively optimize $\btheta$ to minimize:
    \begin{equation}\label{c_theta_objective_finite}
        \min_{\btheta} \mathop{\mathbb{E}}\left[ \hat{C}_T(\btheta)\right].
    \end{equation}

We might wonder about the structure of the optimization problem represented by \eqref{c_theta_objective_finite}, i.e. how do expected costs change as a function of the policy parameters $\btheta$. Since costs \eqref{lost_sales_cost} are a convex function of the starting inventories $I_t$, perhaps they are also well-behaved as a function of $\btheta$. It appears that as a function of $\btheta$, costs are highly non-convex with many local optima (see \ref{appendix:non_convexity_}), calling for advanced optimization methods that shall be explored in the next section.

\section{How to find the parameters of the policy function approximator?}\label{policy_optimization}
There are two primary methods for optimizing the parameters of a policy function approximator: Policy Gradient and derivative-free methods. Policy gradient methods are a category of RL techniques that optimize the parameter vector $\btheta$ of the policy function approximator using gradient descent. On the other hand, derivative-free methods update the parameter vector $\btheta$ using an evolutionary framework. There are various derivative-free methods available in the literature, and in this paper, we adopt Evolution Strategies (ES), which is a population-based stochastic optimization methodology. ES belongs to the family of evolutionary algorithms such as the Genetic Algorithm. Although it has traditionally been used for optimizing low-dimensional continuous optimization problems, recent studies have shown that it can also be scaled to optimize neural networks with several million parameters \citep{Salimans2017EvolutionLearning}.


In general, the evolutionary optimization framework for policy optimization operates as follows. We assume that the parameter vector $\btheta$ of the policy function approximator follows a certain distribution, usually a multivariate Gaussian distribution. At each iteration of the optimization, a population of policy function approximators is sampled from this distribution. Next, the performance of each policy function approximator in solving the problem is evaluated.  Finally, the underlying distribution of $\btheta$, represented by $\psi$, is updated based on the performance of each policy function approximator within the population.
\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Evolutionary optimization framework}
\label{es_framework}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
     \STATE \textbf{Input:} population size $N$, parameter distribution $\psi$:
      \FOR{iteration $i=1,2,\ldots$}
       \STATE Sample a population of parameters $\btheta^{(n)}_i$ for $ n  =  1, 2,  \dots, N$ from distribution $\mathds{P}_\psi(\btheta)$
        \STATE Compute the average cost $\bar{C}_T\left(\btheta^{(n)}_i\right)$ of the problem for $ n  =  1, 2,  \dots, N$
        \STATE Update the parameters of the underlying distribution denoted by $\psi$ based on the computed costs
      \ENDFOR
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

To better illustrate the evolutionary optimization framework, let us consider an example using the 12-dimensional linear function approximator introduced in Section \ref{policy_function_app}. In this case, assume that $\btheta$ follows a multivariate Gaussian distribution with a mean vector $\bmu$ of size 12 and a fixed covariance matrix of $\sigma^2 \mathbb{I}$, where $\mathbb{I}$ is the $12 \times 12$ identity matrix. We set the population size of Evolution Strategies (ES) to $N=50$.

At the first iteration of ES, we randomly sample 50 different 12-dimensional vectors from the distribution $\mathcal{N}(\bmu, \sigma^2 \mathbb{I})$, which we denote as $\btheta^{(n)}_1$ for $n \in \{1, 2, \dots, 50\}$. This results in 50 distinct policy function approximators. We then evaluate the performance of each policy function approximator in solving the lost sales problem, using their respective average-cost-per-period values denoted by $\bar{C}_T\left(\btheta^{(n)}_1\right)$ for some appropriate value of $T$. Finally, we update the mean vector $\bmu$ of the underlying Gaussian distribution based on these costs. This process is repeated until a stopping criterion, such as a fixed number of iterations, is met.


There are various ES techniques available in literature that differ in how they represent the distribution of $\btheta$ and update its parameters. In this section, we first introduce a variant presented in \cite{Salimans2017EvolutionLearning} for the sake of simplicity. This method is a straightforward approach used to optimize neural networks with millions of parameters for solving Atari games and MuJoCo physics simulators. It models the population distribution as a Gaussian distribution with a fixed diagonal covariance matrix. Then, we introduce CMA-ES, which is one of the most advanced ES variants and the one we adopt in our numerical experiments. CMA-ES models the underlying distribution of $\btheta$ using a multivariate Gaussian distribution with a full covariance matrix, as described in \citep{Hansen2001CompletelyStrategies.}.

\subsection{Simple Gaussian Evolution Strategies}
In this section, we introduce the Simple Gaussian Evolution Strategies (ES), which differs from other evolutionary methods, such as the Cross Entropy method and genetic algorithm, by using the entire population to estimate a gradient signal for updating the population distribution. This variant assumes that the underlying distribution of the population follows a $D$-dimensional isotropic Gaussian distribution, denoted as $\mathds{P}_\psi(\btheta)$, where $D$ is the length of $\btheta$, and $\psi$ represents the mean $\bmu$ of the Gaussian distribution.

Specifically, the distribution is given by:

\begin{equation}
\mathds{P}_\psi(\btheta) \sim \mathcal{N}(\bmu, \sigma^2 \mathbb{I}) = \bmu + \sigma \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbb{I}),
\end{equation}

where $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of the distribution, which can be either fixed or treated as an additional parameter.

The input to ES includes the initial mean of the distribution ($\bmu_1 = \mathbf{0}$), the standard deviation of the distribution ($\sigma$), and the learning rate $\alpha\in [0,1]$ for updating the mean. At each iteration of the algorithm, we first sample perturbations to the parameters of the policy network, calculate the cost of each perturbation, and then update the mean of the distribution. We use the update procedure described in Algorithm \ref{nes} \citep{Salimans2017EvolutionLearning}.
 \begin{algorithm}
\caption{Simple Gaussian evolution strategies framework}
\label{nes}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \STATE \textbf{Input:} Initial policy parameters $\bmu_1$, noise standard deviation $\sigma$, learning rate $\alpha$
    \STATE \textbf{Output:} Gaussian distribution parameters $\psi$
      \FOR{iteration $i = 1, 2, 3, \dots$}
        \STATE Sample $\bepsilon^1, \dots, \bepsilon^N$ from $\mathcal{N}(0,\,\mathbb{I})\,$
        \STATE Compute costs $C^n=\bar{C}_T\left(\btheta^{(n)}_i\right)$ for $\btheta^n_i = \bmu_i + \sigma\bepsilon^n$ for $n = 1, \dots, N$
        \STATE Set $\bmu_{i+1} \leftarrow \bmu_i + \alpha\frac{1}{N\sigma}\sum\limits_{n=1}^NC^n\bepsilon^n$
      \ENDFOR
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategies}

CMA-ES is a method that models the population distribution as a multivariate Gaussian distribution with mean $\bmu$, covariance matrix $\bSigma$, and standard deviation $\sigma$. This is expressed mathematically as:
\begin{equation}\label{cma_gaussian_dist}
        \mathds{P}_\psi(\btheta) \sim \mathcal{N}(\bmu, \sigma^2 \bSigma) = \bmu + \sigma \mathcal{N}(0, \bSigma).
\end{equation}
The main difference between CMA-ES and simpler variants of evolutionary strategies (ES) is in how the mean, standard deviation, and covariance matrix of the distribution are updated. To update the mean of the population distribution in CMA-ES, a weighted average of $N^\prime$ selected individuals is used. The equation for this update is given as follows:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \bmu_{i+1} = \sum\limits_{n^\prime=1}^{N^\prime} w^{n^\prime}\btheta^{n^\prime}_{i+1} &, \\
  \sum\limits_{n^\prime=1}^{N^\prime} w^{n^\prime} = 1 &,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $w^{n^\prime}$ is the weight associated with the individual with the $n^\prime$-th best reward or cost. Typically, $N^\prime$ is set to half of the population, and $w^{n^\prime} = N^\prime - n^\prime + 1$ is used as a simple heuristic for setting the weights. These weights are then normalized to ensure they sum to one. For more advanced discussions on setting and normalizing weights, readers are referred to \cite{Hansen2016TheTutorial}.

It is important to note that the current mean of the population is updated using samples from the population distribution in the previous iteration. However, this update procedure may not be suitable for problems with noisy rewards. To address this, a learning rate $\alpha_\mu$ can be added to the update procedure, as shown in Equation (\ref{cma_alpha_mean}):
\begin{equation}\label{cma_alpha_mean}
  \bmu_{i+1} = \bmu_i + \alpha_\mu\sum\limits_{n^\prime=1}^{N^\prime} w^{n^\prime}\left(\btheta^{n^\prime}_{i+1} - \bmu_i\right).
\end{equation}

In CMA-ES, the covariance matrix is also updated using the $N^\prime$ best individuals in the population, similar to the mean update. The covariance matrix update equation is shown in Equation (\ref{cma_cov}).
\begin{equation}\label{cma_cov}
  \bSigma_{i+1} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2_i}\sum\limits_{n^\prime=1}^{N^\prime} w^{n^\prime}\left(\btheta^{n^\prime}_{i+1} - \bmu_i\right)\left(\btheta^{n^\prime}_{i+1} - \bmu_i\right)^\intercal.
\end{equation}
Letting $\bepsilon^n_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}\btheta^{n}_{i+1} - \bmu_i$,  we can rewrite the covariance matrix update equation as a function of $\bepsilon^n_i$ given in Equation (\ref{cma_cov_2}).
\begin{equation}\label{cma_cov_2}
  \bSigma_{i+1} = \sum\limits_{n^\prime=1}^{N^\prime} w^{n^\prime}\bepsilon_i^{n^\prime} (\bepsilon_i^{n^\prime})^\intercal.
\end{equation}
It is important to note that $\bepsilon$ follows a Gaussian distribution with a mean of 0 and a covariance matrix of $\bSigma$. In order to calculate the parameters of the policy network, we sample the population from this distribution using Equation (\ref{cma_gaussian_dist}). However, it is worth mentioning that updating the covariance matrix using Equation (\ref{cma_cov_2}) may not always work effectively for a CMA-ES with a small population size. Therefore, \cite{Hansen2016TheTutorial} proposes more advanced methods for updating the covariance matrix, which are implemented in \cite{Hansen2019CMA-ES/pycmaGithub} and used in our optimization procedure.

In particular, this CMA-ES implementation employs several advanced techniques for adapting the covariance matrix over iterations. In particular, it uses a rank-one update based on an evolution path—an accumulated direction of successive successful steps—to preserve directional memory and avoid premature loss of variance in important directions. This update is combined with a rank-$\mu$ update, which uses the weighted sum of the top-performing individuals to adjust the covariance matrix toward more promising regions of the search space. Both updates are applied using exponential smoothing to balance exploration and exploitation.

Additionally, the CMA-ES algorithm includes a mechanism for step-size adaptation using the cumulative step-length adaptation (CSA) rule. This approach maintains a separate evolution path for the step size and adjusts it depending on whether recent steps align with the expected path length. If steps are consistently longer than expected, the algorithm increases the step size to promote global search; otherwise, it decreases the step size to fine-tune local search. These mechanisms are crucial for enabling CMA-ES to remain effective in high-dimensional, noisy, and ill-conditioned optimization landscapes.

\item \textbf{Sample Efficiency}: Our proposed CMA-ES approach evaluates all candidate policies for an episode using a single demand sequence, providing a fair comparison between policies. \subsection{Managerial Insights and Practical Implementation}
\label{managerial_insights}
Beyond the performance metrics, our study yields managerial insights with direct practical implications. First, simple linear policies can be sufficient to solve complex inventory problems. This challenges the idea that inventory optimization necessarily requires either elaborate problem‑specific heuristics or large, black‑box neural networks. In particular, linear policies optimized with CMA‑ES achieve near‑optimal performance even in challenging scenarios with long lead times.

Second, linear policies offer concrete implementation advantages. They can be deployed in standard spreadsheet software or simple ERP system modules without specialized programming knowledge or heavy libraries. At run time, a linear policy reduces to a single small‑scale matrix multiplication, which makes deployment straightforward on virtually any computing platform. The mapping from state variables to order decisions is also transparent, which helps explainability for non‑technical stakeholders and supports adoption in practice.

Third, training requirements are modest. Linear policies contain far fewer parameters—typically tens to a few hundreds—than common deep neural network approaches, and training can be performed on standard desktop hardware. This lowers both the computational and organizational barriers to experimentation and deployment.

Taken together, these observations indicate that, with strong optimization methods such as CMA‑ES, practitioners can attain excellent performance using simple, transparent, and practical policy structures.


\section{Conclusions}
\label{conclusions}

Recently, there has been significant attention towards training Reinforcement Learning agents for inventory management problems. However, RL has some drawbacks that need to be resolved for successful applications, such as the need for extensive hyperparameter optimization. This paper proposes using an alternative policy optimization method, namely CMA-ES to address this problem. Specifically, we demonstrate how to train linear and neural network policy function approximators for the lost sales problem using CMA-ES. Compared to RL, CMA-ES has fewer hyperparameters, requires less hyperparameter optimization, and can train agents that outperform heuristics such as Myopic-2 and Capped Base-Stock policy for problems with a shortage cost of 4, and result in comparable performance overall. Both linear and neural network policy function approximators can be used with CMA-ES.

\section{On the Cost as a Function of the Policy Network Parameters}
\label{appendix:non_convexity_}

In this paper, we study neural network policies for the lost sales inventory problem, and in particular we propose the linear policy: A special case of a neural network policy that does not have any hidden layers. One may wonder about the structure of the costs as a function of the parameters of our linear policy. In this appendix, we demonstrate that costs are a complex function of the network parameters, with no apparent structure such as convexity.

In particular, we identify a counter-example to convexity that simultaneously demonstrates the complex behavior of costs as a function of the network parameters. This non-convexity helps explain why gradient-free methods like CMA-ES are more appropriate for this optimization problem than gradient-based methods that might get trapped in local optima.

If the cost function were convex, then for any two points in the parameter space, the cost of any convex combination of these points should be no greater than the convex combination of their costs.

We examine three specific points in the parameter space:

\item Point A: Near-optimal policy with a cost = 4.78
\begin{itemize}
\item Weights:

\subsection{Analysis of Non-Convexity}

This counter-example demonstrates clear violation of convexity:

\begin{itemize}
\item Point A achieves a near-optimal average cost of 4.78
\item Point B, a random perturbation, maintains a reasonable cost of 8.49
\item Point C, a weighted linear combination (0.2A + 0.8B), performs significantly worse with an average cost of 17.50
\end{itemize}

If the cost function were convex, the cost at point C should be at most $0.2 \times 4.78 + 0.8 \times 8.49 = 7.75$. However, the actual cost at C is 17.50, which is 2.26 times higher.

Even more tellingly, point C's performance is close to what one would expect from a policy with completely random parameters, demonstrating that even small movements in the parameter space can lead to dramatic performance deterioration. This empirical evidence reveals a highly non-convex optimization landscape with multiple local optima and complex behavior.

\subsection{Implications for Optimization}

This non-convexity explains why gradient-free methods like CMA-ES are well-suited for this problem:

\begin{itemize}
\item Gradient-based methods can easily get trapped in local optima in such a landscape
\item The policy parameter space contains local minima and maxima that make gradient-based exploration challenging
\item Population-based methods like CMA-ES can more effectively explore this complex parameter space
\item The adaptive nature of CMA-ES helps navigate the irregular optimization landscape
\end{itemize}

Furthermore, these results highlight the mathematical complexity of the lost-sales inventory problem, even when using relatively simple linear policy structures. The non-convexity arises from the inherent dynamics of the lost-sales setting, where the truncation of demand and the interaction with pipeline inventory create complex state transitions that manifest as an irregular cost landscape in the policy parameter space.
