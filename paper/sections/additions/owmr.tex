\paragraph{New regimes: backorder and lost sales.}
The instances above are all partial-backorder. To test whether the same per-retailer
soft tree carries to qualitatively different demand-fulfilment dynamics, we add three
$K{=}3$ instances of \citet{kaynov2024owmr} drawn from the other two regimes of
Table~A.3: a \emph{full-backorder} instance with heterogeneous high-variability demand
($N(1,5),N(5,1),\mathrm{Pois}(0.5)$; $L_w{=}2$, $L_r{=}1$), a \emph{lost-sales} instance
with \emph{asymmetric retailer lead times} ($L_r{=}1,2,3$ over symmetric $\mathrm{Pois}(3)$
demand; $L_w{=}2$), and a \emph{lost-sales} instance with heterogeneous demand and
\emph{long lead times} ($L_w{=}5$, $L_r{=}3$). The three smaller $K{=}3$ instances keep
the gate's full Cartesian grid tractable. Table~\ref{tab:owmr-results-regimes} reports the
results under the identical protocol of Table~\ref{tab:owmr-results}.

On all three the learned per-retailer soft tree \emph{matches} (ties) the tuned in-repo
gate to within numerical error rather than beating it: with the per-retailer warm-start
floor the trained CMA-ES incumbent lands above the strong gate on the held-out block, so
the deployed policy falls back to the gate-reproducing anchor (paired advantage $0.00$, not
exceeding its standard error). Across these regimes the published PPO of
\citet{kaynov2024owmr} is \emph{cheaper} than both the learned policy and the heuristic gate
($-1.05\%$, $-1.96\%$, $-6.43\%$ for the learned policy relative to PPO), so the learned
policy stays below PPO here; we make no PPO claim. The takeaway is one of \emph{robustness}:
the per-retailer geometry does not degrade when moved from partial backorder to full
backorder or lost sales (including under lead-time asymmetry and long leads), reproducing the
strongest in-repo heuristic exactly; the genuine held-out \emph{beats} of
Table~\ref{tab:owmr-results} remain confined to the heterogeneous and high-CV
partial-backorder instances where there is dynamic structure the static gate cannot express.
