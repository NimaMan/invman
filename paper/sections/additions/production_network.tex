\paragraph{Topology diversification.}
To check that the result is not an artefact of the serial chain, we re-ran the \emph{same} recipe and
protocol (a depth-2 oblique soft tree with linear leaves, identical population, generations, and
held-out paired-common-random-number evaluation; no re-sweep) on two further topologies of the same
supply-network model: a \emph{mixed distribution-and-assembly network} (an external source feeds a
single node that \emph{distributes} to two intermediate nodes, whose output two downstream
\emph{assembly} nodes each combine to serve a customer) and a \emph{pure assembly network} (a
three-layer assembly tree in which four sources feed two intermediate assembly nodes that feed a final
assembly node serving the customer). Both move the action space well beyond the serial chain: the
distribution split and the assembly $\min(\cdot)$ production starve downstream stages, so the
environment's own best pairwise base-stock needs much higher order-up-to levels (gates $297.69$ and
$283.34$ per period, against $60.24$ for the serial case). On the pure assembly network the learned
tree beats this gate by $-2.98\%$ ($274.90\pm0.17$ vs $283.34$, outside the held-out standard error by
$\approx40\times$); on the mixed network it is at parity-to-marginally-better, beating the gate by up
to $-0.99\%$ ($294.73\pm0.92$ vs $297.69$) on the best of three CMA seeds while a third seed sits
$+2.9\%$ above it. As for the serial case, these comparators are the \emph{environment's own}
grid-searched best heuristic on a faithful but \texttt{literature\_verified=false} environment --- the
source paper gives no analytical optimum for mixed or assembly networks (only random-search, DFO,
Spearmint, and DNN benchmarks) --- so these are research learned-beats-(or-matches)-own-heuristic
results across serial, mixed distribution-plus-assembly, and pure-assembly structures, and are
\emph{not} published-number beats.
