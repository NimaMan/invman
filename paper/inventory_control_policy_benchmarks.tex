\section{Lost-sales inventory control}

\subsection{Problem description}

We study single-item lost-sales inventory control and its fixed-ordering-cost variant. Each period
$t$ the controller observes the inventory state --- the on-hand level and the outstanding-order
pipeline $(q_{t-L+1},\dots,q_{t-1})$ --- and chooses an integer replenishment order
$q_t\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ that arrives after a fixed lead time $L$. Demand $D_t$ is stochastic;
sales are limited to on-hand stock and \emph{unmet demand is lost} rather than backordered. With
on-hand stock $x_t$ available to serve demand $D_t$, the period ends with inventory
$I_t=(x_t-D_t)^{+}$ and lost sales $(D_t-x_t)^{+}$ (writing $(\cdot)^{+}=\max(\cdot,0)$), incurring
the one-period cost
\[
c_t = h\,(x_t-D_t)^{+} + p\,(D_t-x_t)^{+},
\]
a holding cost $h$ per unit left on hand and a penalty $p$ per unit of lost sales.

\textbf{Fixed-cost lost sales} is a simple variant of the same problem: the dynamics, state, and
lost-sales rule are identical, and the one-period cost gains a single term
\[
c_t^{K} = c_t + K\,\mathbf{1}\{q_t>0\},
\]
a fixed cost $K$ charged whenever a positive order is placed. This term makes the
\emph{order/no-order} decision --- whether to replenish at all --- the dominant one, and it is
precisely on this decision that the action parameterizations below differ most.

\subsection{Policy functions}

The action space is a property of the policy, not of the environment. Every policy maps the state
feature vector $x_t\in\mathbb{R}^d$ to a nonnegative integer order $a_t\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$, and
some decoders introduce a policy-side cap $\bar q$ (a design choice, not an environment
restriction); for the pipeline state the feature dimension is $d=L$. The environment emits the raw
state and the policy applies its own divide-by-scale normalization before acting. We write $\sigma$
for the logistic function, $\mathrm{softplus}(z)=\ln(1+e^{z})$, and $\Pi$ for
nearest-nonnegative-integer rounding. Each policy composes a \emph{backbone} that produces latent
outputs with a \emph{decoder} that turns them into the order. We use two backbones --- a linear map
$u_\theta(x)=Wx+b$ and a one-hidden-layer network $u_\theta(x)=V\phi(Ux+c)+e$ of width $H=8$ with
SELU activation $\phi$ --- and the reported benchmark surface pairs them with three decoders.

\paragraph{Soft-gated direct quantity (L-SGD, NN-SGD).}
The backbone emits a gate score $g(x)$ and a quantity score $q(x)$, and the order is the gated
softplus quantity
\[
a_t = \Pi\!\big(\sigma(g(x_t))\,\mathrm{softplus}(q(x_t))\big).
\]

\paragraph{Soft-gated ordinal quantity (L-SGO, NN-SGO).}
The backbone emits a gate score $g(x)$ and $\bar q$ ordinal threshold scores
$o_1(x),\dots,o_{\bar q}(x)$, and the order gates a sum of threshold probabilities,
\[
a_t = \Pi\!\Big(\sigma(g(x_t))\sum_{k=1}^{\bar q}\sigma(o_k(x_t))\Big),
\]
so the head can express any quantity in $\{0,\dots,\bar q\}$ but, unlike the direct head, its
output dimension (and parameter count) grows with $\bar q$.

\paragraph{Soft trees with linear leaves (Tree-1, Tree-2).}
A soft decision tree with oblique splits computes leaf path-probabilities $\pi_\ell(x)$; each leaf
emits a local softplus-affine quantity $q_\ell(x)=\mathrm{softplus}(\alpha_\ell^\top x+\beta_\ell)$,
and the order is the rounded mixture
\[
a_t = \Pi\!\Big(\sum_\ell \pi_\ell(x_t)\,q_\ell(x_t)\Big).
\]
We report depth-1 (Tree-1) and depth-2 (Tree-2) trees.

For the active $L=4$ pipeline state ($d=4$, $d+1=5$) the trainable parameter counts are compact and,
for the direct and tree heads, independent of the cap: the linear and network direct heads carry
$2(d+1)=10$ and $H(d+1)+2(H+1)=58$ parameters respectively, and the depth-1 and depth-2 trees carry
$3(d+1)=15$ and $7(d+1)=35$. Only the ordinal heads scale with the cap, at $(\bar q+1)(d+1)$ for
L-SGO and $H(d+1)+(\bar q+1)(H+1)$ for NN-SGO, which evaluates to $105$ and $229$ at the vanilla cap
$\bar q=20$ and to $255$ and $499$ at the fixed-cost cap $\bar q=50$.

\subsection{Experiment setup}

\paragraph{Instances.}
We benchmark on the full paper grid rather than a single instance. The core parameters are holding
cost $h=1$, mean demand $\mu=5$, lead times $L\in\{4,6,8,10\}$, and lost-sales penalties
$p\in\{4,19\}$, crossed with the four demand families below, giving $4\times4\times2=32$ vanilla
instances. The fixed-cost grid additionally crosses the fixed cost $K\in\{5,25\}$, giving $64$
instances.

\paragraph{Demand families.}
The four families are mean-preserving, all with $\mathbb{E}[D_t]=5$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Poisson demand with mean $5$,
    \item Geometric demand with mean $5$,
    \item positively correlated two-state Markov-modulated Poisson (MMPP2$+$) demand with
    $(\lambda_{\mathrm{low}},\lambda_{\mathrm{high}})=(3,7)$ and $p_{00}=p_{11}=0.9$,
    \item negatively correlated (MMPP2$-$) demand with the same rates and $p_{00}=p_{11}=0.1$.
\end{itemize}
For the MMPP2 cases the latent regime $S_t\in\{\mathrm{low},\mathrm{high}\}$ follows a two-state
Markov chain with stay-probabilities $p_{00},p_{11}$, and $D_t\mid S_t\sim
\mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_{S_t})$. Because $p_{00}=p_{11}$ the stationary regime probabilities are
$1/2$ each, preserving the mean $\tfrac12(3)+\tfrac12(7)=5$. The temporal dependence is set by the
regime eigenvalue $\rho_{\mathrm{regime}}=p_{00}+p_{11}-1=\pm0.8$; after Poisson observation noise
the implemented lag-one demand autocorrelation is
\[
\mathrm{Corr}(D_t,D_{t+1})
=\frac{\pi_{\mathrm{low}}\pi_{\mathrm{high}}(\lambda_{\mathrm{high}}-\lambda_{\mathrm{low}})^2
(p_{00}+p_{11}-1)}{\mathrm{Var}(D_t)}=\pm\frac{3.2}{9}\approx\pm0.356,
\]
positive for the persistent case and sign-alternating for the alternating case, whereas the Poisson
and Geometric families are i.i.d.\ (zero autocorrelation at every lag).

\paragraph{Training and evaluation.}
Policies are trained with CMA-ES (population $64$, training horizon $2000$) and evaluated on the
long-run average cost over horizon $10^6$ with $10$ seeds and base seed $42$. The benchmark
heuristics are the myopic-1, myopic-2, and smoothed value-based base-stock (SVBS) policies for
vanilla lost sales, and the $(s,S)$, $(s,nQ)$, and modified $(s,S,q)$ policies for the fixed-cost
variant, using literature-reported parameters where available.

Two readings stand out. Vanilla lost sales is comparatively forgiving: across the i.i.d.\ families
the soft-gated direct head and the soft trees land in essentially the same good basin, and the
learned policies match or beat the best heuristic on almost every trained instance. Fixed-cost lost
sales is the sharper architecture test, with no single dominant policy and a relative ordering of
the heads that shifts with the demand family and the fixed cost $K$. The driving factor is not raw
representational capacity but search geometry: under a fixed setup cost the action parameterization
determines which order/no-order boundary CMA-ES can actually reach. The policies that win are those
whose parameterization makes the relevant replenishment map easy enough for CMA-ES to find within
the available budget.

\section{Dual sourcing}
\label{sec:dual-sourcing}

\subsection{Problem description}

We study the dual-sourcing inventory model of \citet{gijsbrechts2022drl}: a single item is
replenished from a slow \emph{regular} supplier with lead time $l_r$ and a fast \emph{expedited}
supplier with lead time $l_e=0$, against i.i.d.\ demand. Each period the controller chooses a
regular order $q^r_t$ (unit cost $c_r$) and an expedited order $q^e_t$ (unit cost $c_e>c_r$);
end-of-period net inventory incurs a holding cost $h$ per unit on hand or a backorder penalty $b$
per unit short. Because only the expedite lead time is zero, the relevant post-decision state
reduces to the net inventory plus the outstanding regular-order pipeline
$(I_t, q^r_{t-l_r+1},\dots,q^r_{t-1})$. The classical structured policies are the single-index,
dual-index \citep{veeraraghavan2008dualindex}, capped dual-index, and tailored base-surge
heuristics \citep{sheopuri2010dualsourcing}.

\subsection{Policy functions}

Here too the action space is a property of the policy we design. The learned policy is a soft tree
on the reduced pipeline state whose leaves emit a control in the \emph{factorized
capped-dual-index coordinates}
\[
(s_e,\ \Delta_r,\ \bar c_r),\qquad s_r=s_e+\Delta_r,
\]
where $s_e$ is the expedite order-up-to level, $s_r$ the regular order-up-to level, and $\bar c_r$ a
cap on the regular order: the expedited order raises net inventory toward $s_e$, and the regular
order raises the inventory position toward $s_r$ subject to the cap. We compare two geometries, a
\emph{small-cap} variant with $\bar c_r$ on the discrete grid $\{1,2,3,4,6,8,12\}$ (axis-aligned,
constant leaves) and a continuous capped-dual-index-targets variant. Both keep the learned policy
in the heuristic's coordinate system while letting the targets depend on state, whereas raw
direct-order outputs cannot express the capped dual-index control. Because the best \emph{static}
control in these coordinates is the capped dual-index (below), we \emph{warm-start} CMA-ES at the
encoded capped-dual-index solution and let it refine around that point; without the warm start,
CMA-ES occasionally lands in a worse basin (the $c_e=110$ rows degrade by $\sim0.5\%$). The wins are
thus a matter of action geometry and reliable optimization rather than extra tree capacity.

\subsection{Experiment setup}

We use the six small-scale benchmark instances of \citet{gijsbrechts2022drl} (their Figure~9),
with $l_r\in\{2,3,4\}$ and $c_e\in\{105,110\}$ and the common parameters of
Table~\ref{tab:ds-params}. The published comparators are the optimality gaps, relative to the
bounded-DP optimum, of the single-index, dual-index, capped dual-index, and tailored base-surge
heuristics together with the A3C learner of \citet{gijsbrechts2022drl}: the capped dual-index gap
is $\le0.11\%$ on every row, making it both the best heuristic and an effective optimal proxy,
whereas A3C ranges from $0.51\%$ to $1.85\%$. We therefore benchmark the learned policy against the
\emph{capped dual-index} cost as the optimal proxy rather than our own bounded DP

under a common-random-number protocol --- the same $70$ demand-path seeds and horizon $6\times10^4$

is statistically meaningful (paired SEM $\sim0.002$--$0.006$ versus per-seed standard deviation

\section{Multi-echelon inventory control: one warehouse, $K$ retailers with special delivery}

\subsection{Problem description}

We study the divergent two-echelon system of \citet{vanroy1997ndp}, also used as a deep-RL
benchmark by \citet{gijsbrechts2022drl}: a single capacitated warehouse replenishes $K$ identical
capacitated retailers. The warehouse orders from an external manufacturer with lead time $l_w$ and
ships to retailers with lead time $l_r$. Each period $t$: (i) the warehouse places an order; (ii)
each retailer raises its inventory position toward a target, shipped from current warehouse stock
(allocated to minimize the spread of retailer positions when warehouse stock is short); (iii)
retailer demand $d_i$ is realized and served from retailer on-hand; (iv) each unmet unit requests a
same-day \emph{special delivery} from leftover warehouse stock with probability $P_w$ and is
otherwise lost --- a hybrid of backlogging and lost sales; (v) pipelines advance. The state is the
warehouse and per-retailer on-hand levels and outstanding-order pipelines. With $I^w_t,I^i_t$ the
end-of-period on-hand inventories and $E_t$ the number of expedited units, the period cost is
\[
c_t = h^w I^w_t + h^r \sum_{i=1}^{K} I^i_t + c^w E_t + p\,(\text{lost units}),
\]
with warehouse/retailer holding costs $h^w,h^r$, unit expedite cost $c^w$, and lost-sale penalty
$p$. Capacities bound the per-period production ($C^m$) and the warehouse and retail inventory
positions ($C^w,C^r$). This is a member of the broader divergent / partial-lost-sales multi-echelon
family \citep{nahmias1994partial, federgruen1984multilocation, dekok2018typology, kaynov2024owmr}.

\subsection{Policy functions}

The action space is a property of the policy we design, not a fixed external grid. We train for the
problem itself and use the published numbers only as a benchmark, independently of the learner in
\citet{gijsbrechts2022drl}. The policy is a soft decision tree with oblique splits and linear
leaves. It consumes the \emph{pure decision state} (the raw, unnormalized warehouse and retailer
on-hand and pipeline entries) and applies a \emph{policy-owned} divide-by-scale normalization before
acting, mirroring our lost-sales policy interface. The two leaf outputs map to a state-dependent
warehouse order-up-to level $y^w_{S_t}$ and a shared retailer order-up-to level $y^r_{S_t}$: the
warehouse order is $q^w_t = \big[\min(y^w_{S_t},C^w) - \mathrm{IP}^w_t\big]^+$ capped by $C^m$, and
each retailer order raises its position toward $\min(y^r_{S_t},C^r)$.

We compare two action designs as a search dimension. The \textbf{grid} design draws the levels from
the reduced discrete set used by \citet{gijsbrechts2022drl}, $y^w\in\{50,60,\dots,100\}$ and
$y^r\in\{0,5,\dots,40\}$, whereas the \textbf{direct} design (ours) lets the leaves estimate the
levels as continuous values rounded to non-negative integers, bounded only by the physical caps
$y^w\in[0,C^w]$ and $y^r\in[0,C^r]$. The distinction matters because the reduced grid caps the
warehouse level at $100$, yet the cost-minimizing warehouse base-stock for these settings is
$\approx 300$--$525$ (the published constant base-stock uses $y^w=330,460$); the grid therefore
cannot express the operating region and is a policy artifact, not a problem constraint.

\subsection{Experiment setup}

We evaluate on \emph{both} multi-echelon settings of \citet{gijsbrechts2022drl} --- the two complex
case studies of \citet{vanroy1997ndp} --- which are the only multi-echelon instances they report, so
our coverage of their multi-echelon benchmark is complete. Table~\ref{tab:me-params} reproduces
their Table~3 parameters exactly for both settings under our faithful \texttt{gijs\_2022} dynamics;
retailer demand is $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$ rounded to a non-negative integer.

\section{Dual-sourcing comparator and the bounded-DP optimum}
\label{app:ds-validation}

For dual sourcing we benchmark against the capped dual-index heuristic as the optimal proxy rather
than a computed optimum. An exhaustive grid search over the capped-dual-index control
$(s_e,\Delta_r,\bar c_r)$ under the evaluation protocol recovers the published capped-dual-index
parameters and confirms that it is the best static control on every Figure~9 row, consistent with
its published $\le0.11\%$ optimality gap. We do \emph{not} use our own bounded-DP value iteration as
the denominator: at inventory bounds that keep the $l_r=4$ state space tractable, the truncated
bounded-DP cost is mildly inconsistent --- it falls slightly below the simulated heuristic costs ---
and widening the bounds to remove the truncation is computationally prohibitive at $l_r=4$. The
capped dual-index proxy is exact to within its published gap and is evaluated on the identical
common-random-number rollout as the learned policies.
