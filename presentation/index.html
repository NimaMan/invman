Inventory control with compact learned policies

Small Structured Policies for Inventory Control

CMA-ES learns interpretable inventory policies when the action decoder lives in the right control coordinates.

Nima Manaf Conference deck Lost sales · dual sourcing · multi-echelon
Open by positioning the work as a representation result, not a generic RL-vs-ES contest.

The control problem

Inventory decisions look simple until lead times create memory.

Every period asks for one operational action: order, split, route, or set a target. The state remembers what is already in transit.

  • Lead-time pipelines make the state grow with history.
  • Shortages can be lost, backordered, or expedited.
  • Network structure makes raw action spaces misleading.

One recipe

CMA-ES optimizes the realized operating cost directly.

The policy is intentionally small. The search distribution explores policy parameters; the simulator supplies the objective.

The same loop handles a scalar order, a regular-expedite pair, or echelon order-up-to levels.

The key design choice

The action parameterization is part of the policy.

A raw action head asks the learner to rediscover the control law and the coordinate transform. A structured decoder searches where good controls already live.

Problem 1

Lost sales: a request stream hits one stock buffer.

The controller chooses one integer order. Demand that cannot be served disappears and incurs a shortage penalty.

Actionscalar order qt
Stateon-hand + lead-time pipeline
ComparatorMyopic-1, Myopic-2, SVBS

Lost-sales mechanics

One action, two exits.

The policy orders into a delayed supply pipeline. After demand arrives, stock either serves the customer or demand leaks into a lost-sales sink.

  • Order quantity must be a non-negative integer.
  • The hard part is timing replenishment under lead-time uncertainty.
  • The cost balances leftover stock against lost demand.

Why the scalar case still matters

The decoder controls what the learner can say.

Direct quantity

Emit an order size, then round and clip.

Ordinal quantity

Ask many soft "one more unit?" questions and sum them.

Gate + quantity

Separate "should I order?" from "how much?".

Soft tree

Route state softly through interpretable decision leaves.

Lost sales is the cleanest teaching example because the action is simple enough to see the decoder effect directly.

Problem 2

Dual sourcing: the action is a pair, but the useful coordinates are not raw orders.

The controller balances a cheap slow regular source against an expensive fast expedited source.

Regularcheap, slow pipeline
Expeditecostly, fast correction
Comparatorcapped dual-index

Dual-sourcing mechanics

Two supply arcs serve one inventory position.

A direct (regular, expedited) action is valid, but it makes the learner rediscover the inventory-position logic that CDI already encodes.

  • Regular orders rebuild inventory cheaply after a delay.
  • Expedite orders respond quickly to shortfall.
  • The strong classical comparator acts on positions and caps.

Dual-sourcing geometry

The learned tree is placed in capped-dual-index coordinates.

Raw order head

The tree emits (q_r, q_e) directly.

Valid but poorly aligned with the known policy structure.

CDI-shaped head

The tree modulates (s_e, delta_r, cap_r) around the CDI solution.

Generation zero can reproduce CDI; search learns only small deviations.

Multi-echelon mechanics

Inventory is coordinated across levels.

The policy chooses order-up-to levels. The warehouse replenishes upstream, retailers receive allocations, and urgent unmet demand can use special delivery.

  • The state combines warehouse inventory, retailer inventories, and pipelines.
  • Retailer allocation couples local shortages through the shared warehouse.
  • The useful action is a level, not a small fixed grid label.

What ties the three cases together

The recipe stays fixed; the decoder changes shape.

01

Lost sales

Scalar order decoded as a direct, ordinal, gated, or tree quantity.

02

Dual sourcing

Regular and expedite orders decoded through CDI coordinates.

03

Multi-echelon

Warehouse and retailer decisions decoded as direct order-up-to levels.

The small policy works because it searches in a control language that the inventory system understands.

Takeaways

Structure in the policy beats capacity in the network.

1. A single gradient-free training loop can cover structurally different inventory systems.

2. The decisive design choice is often the action geometry, not backbone width.

3. Honest reporting matters: match optima, beat heuristics, and keep cross-protocol DRL as context.

Limits and next steps

The failures point to the next useful experiments.

Lost-sales misses

The high-penalty, autocorrelated, long-lead MMPP cells remain classical-policy wins.

Dual sourcing ceiling

CDI is already near-optimal in this benchmark, so the right claim is recovery, not improvement.

Multi-echelon comparability

A3C numbers use different baselines and cost conventions, so they stay context-only.

Next step: search the decoder class itself, then pair structured action heads with more sample-efficient learners.
